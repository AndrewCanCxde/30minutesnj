The Problem

A rushed lunch isn't really lunch at all.

When students have only 10–15 minutes to eat after waiting in line, the consequences extend far beyond the cafeteria.

🧠

Academic Performance

Adequate nutrition directly fuels cognitive function. Students who eat full meals show improved focus, memory retention, and classroom engagement throughout the afternoon.

— FRAC: School Meals & Academic Outcomes
🥗

Food Waste & Nutrition

Students with less than 20 minutes to eat consume 13% less of their entrée, 12% less vegetables, and 10% less milk than students with at least 25 minutes — and waste significantly more food.

— Cohen et al., Journal of the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics, 2016
😌

Mental Health & Stress

Food insecurity and rushed meals are linked to elevated anxiety, irritability, and reduced social engagement. Children need adequate mealtime to decompress and connect with peers.

— FRAC: School Meals & Mental Health
⚖️

Equity Issues

Nearly 30 million children rely on the National School Lunch Program daily. For low-income students, school lunch is often their most nutritious meal — a short period means they lose the most.

— FRAC: Benefits of School Lunch
🏃

Physical Health

Eating too quickly is linked to poor digestion, weight gain, and metabolic risk. It takes roughly 20 minutes for the body to signal fullness — students eating in 10 minutes never get that signal.

— Northwestern Medicine
📋

No Federal Minimum Exists

There is no federal law requiring a minimum school lunch period — not even one minute. The CDC recommends 30 minutes total so students have 20 minutes of actual seated eating time, but schools face no legal obligation to follow it.

— CDC: Making Time for School Lunch
The Evidence

The research is clear.

Decades of peer-reviewed studies support a minimum 30-minute seated lunch period for K–12 students.

13%

Less entrée consumed with under 20 minutes to eat

A Harvard study of 1,001 students found that those with less than 20 minutes consumed 13% less of their entrée, 12% less vegetables, and 10% less milk than students with at least 25 minutes — and were significantly less likely to select fruit.

Cohen et al. — Journal of the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics, 2016 →

"Many children, especially those from low-income families, rely on school meals for up to half their daily energy intake — so it is essential that we give students a sufficient amount of time to eat."

— Juliana Cohen, ScD, Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health

30 min

CDC Recommended Lunch Period

The CDC recommends students have at least 20 minutes of seated eating time after receiving their meal. Since line wait time is not included, a total period of 30 minutes is the functional minimum the CDC supports.

CDC: Making Time for School Lunch →
1.3M+

NJ Students Affected

Over 1.3 million students are enrolled in New Jersey public schools — none of them protected by a statewide minimum lunch period law. Every one of them is subject to whatever their district decides, with no floor.

Ballotpedia — NJ Public Education Data →
20 min

Time for Fullness Signals to Register

Northwestern Medicine researchers note that it takes approximately 20 minutes for the body's hormones to signal fullness. Students eating in 10–15 minutes routinely miss this window, contributing to overeating and metabolic risk.

Northwestern Medicine →
2022

NJ S571 — A Precedent

A bipartisan bill (S571) was introduced in NJ in 2022 requiring 20 seated minutes. It was referred to the Senate Education Committee but never advanced. We're picking up where it left off — and going further.

NJ Legislature, S571 →
2023

Other States Have Started — But Not Gone Far Enough

New Mexico enacted the strongest law to date in 2023, requiring 20 minutes of seated lunch time for K–5 students. Minnesota and South Carolina introduced similar bills but both stalled. No state has enacted a 30-minute total period standard — NJ's proposed law would be the most protective in the country.

But laws requiring "seated" time are nearly impossible to enforce — who decides when a student is officially "seated"? Schools can game the definition. A requirement for 30 total minutes from the bell is objective, observable, and enforceable. That's why our proposed law sets a clear 30-minute floor with no ambiguity.

NM SB 4, 2023 →
Our Proposed Legislation

The law we're fighting for.

Drafted by student advocates and modeled after existing New Jersey child labor protections, this is the language we are asking the Legislature to adopt.

"No student under eighteen years of age enrolled in a school other than an institution for higher education shall be required to attend instruction for more than five hours continuously without an interval of at least thirty minutes for a lunch period, and no period of less than thirty minutes shall be deemed to be a fit lunch period for students."
Modeled After
NJ Child Labor Law — existing protections for workers under 18
Applies To
All K–12 public school students in New Jersey
Minimum Standard
30 minutes — no exceptions, no ambiguity
